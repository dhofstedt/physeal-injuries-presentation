Stressed or Fractured?

MRI Differentiation of Physeal Injuries in Children

Daniel Hofstedt, DO

February 11, 2025 | MSK Journal Club

Clinical Context

Pediatric Sports Participation

>30 million U.S. children in organized sports

10% annual injury rate (3 million injuries/year)

High-risk activities:

  • Gymnastics (axial loading)
  • Multi-sport training
  • Contact sports

Physeal Anatomy

Structural Organization

  1. Resting zone
  2. Proliferative zone
  3. Hypertrophic zone

Key Landmark

Zone of Provisional Calcification (ZPC)

Trilaminar MRI Appearance

Trilaminar MRI appearance:

  • Hyperintense physeal cartilage
  • Hypointense ZPC
  • Vascular metaphysis

Pathomechanisms

Chronic Stress Injury

  • Repetitive axial loading (gymnasts)
  • Traction stresses (apophyses)
  • Metaphyseal vascular compromise

Pathomechanisms

Acute Salter-Harris Fracture

  • Transverse physeal cleavage
  • Vertical cartilage disruption
  • Periosteal/perichondrial rupture

MRI Differentiators

Chronic Stress Injury (79% Specific)

  • Focal physeal thickening (42%)
  • ZPC discontinuity (79%)
  • Intact periosteum (98%)

MRI Differentiators

Acute SH Fracture (83% Specific)

  • Periosteal disruption (83%)
  • Perichondrial rupture (80%)
  • Soft tissue edema (97%)

Clinical Challenge

12yo Female Gymnast

  • Chronic: Medial tibial ZPC disruption
  • Acute: Lateral SH III fracture
  • Key markers:
    • Intact vs disrupted periosteum
    • Focal vs generalized thickening

Management Implications

Finding Chronic Stress Acute Fracture
Treatment Activity modification Immobilization
Complications 26% growth disturbance 18% premature fusion

Takeaway Points

  1. ZPC integrity is key differentiator (OR 5.7)
  2. Periosteal injury rare in chronic stress (2%)
  3. Multifocal thickening suggests chronicity

Future Directions

Long-term MRI follow-up may refine diagnostic criteria. Further research is needed to validate these imaging markers.
