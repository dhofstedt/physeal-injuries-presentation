Stressed or Fractured?

MRI Differentiation of Physeal Injuries in Children

Daniel Hofstedt, DO

February 11, 2025 | MSK Journal Club

Background

  • Pediatric athletes: high sports participation
  • Risk of overuse & acute injuries
  • Challenge: Differentiating chronic physeal stress injury vs acute Salter–Harris fracture
  • Incomplete clinical histories at time of imaging

Study Purpose

  • Identify MRI markers for chronic physeal stress injury
  • Differentiation from acute Salter–Harris fracture
  • Focus on knee & wrist injuries

Clinical Context

Pediatric Sports Participation

>30 million U.S. children in organized sports

1 in 10 pediatric sport-related injuries estimated per year

High-risk activities:

  • Gymnastics (axial loading)
  • Multi-sport training
  • Contact sports

Physeal Anatomy

Structural Organization

  1. Resting zone
  2. Proliferative zone
  3. Hypertrophic zone

Key Landmark

Zone of Provisional Calcification (ZPC)

ZPC on MRI includes the histologic ZPC and adjacent mineralized primary spongiosa.

Trilaminar MRI Appearance

Trilaminar appearance:

  • Hyperintense physeal cartilage
  • Hypointense ZPC
  • Vascular metaphysis

Pathomechanisms

Chronic Stress Injury

  • Repetitive axial loading
  • Traction stresses at apophyses
  • Metaphyseal vascular compromise

Pathomechanisms

Acute Salter–Harris Fracture

  • Transverse physeal cleavage
  • Vertical cartilage disruption
  • Periosteal/perichondrial rupture

MRI Differentiators

Chronic Stress Injury Findings

(These percentages indicate the proportion of chronic injury cases showing each finding)

  • Focal physeal thickening in 42% of cases
  • ZPC discontinuity in 79% of cases
  • Intact periosteum in 98% of cases

MRI Differentiators

Acute Salter–Harris Fracture Findings

  • Periosteal disruption in 83% of cases
  • Perichondrial rupture in 80% of cases
  • Soft tissue edema in 97% of cases

Clinical Challenge

12yo Female Gymnast – Lateral Aspect

  • Arrow: Nondisplaced Salter–Harris III fracture of the lateral proximal tibia extending into the physis.
  • Dashed Arrow: Diffuse proximal tibial physeal widening.
  • Curved Arrow: Focal periosteal disruption.
  • Asterisk: Small knee effusion.

12yo Female Gymnast – Medial Aspect

  • Dashed Ovals: Focal physeal thickening, indicating chronic stress injury.
  • Arrowheads: Interruption of the Zone of Provisional Calcification (ZPC).
  • Similar findings are observed on both medial and lateral aspects of the distal femur.

Case Significance

Great case showing both acute and chronic physeal injury findings in a single image.

  • Acute: Periosteal disruption, soft tissue edema.
  • Chronic: Focal physeal thickening, ZPC discontinuity.

Management Implications

Finding Chronic Stress Acute Fracture
Treatment Activity modification Immobilization
Complications 26% growth disturbance 18% premature fusion

Takeaway Points

  1. Loss of ZPC integrity with focal physeal thickening is a key differentiator (Odds Ratio 5.7)

    (The combination of ZPC discontinuity and focal thickening is 5.7 times more likely in chronic stress injuries than in acute SH fractures.)

  1. Periosteal injury is rare in chronic stress (<2%)

    (<2% of chronic stress cases show periosteal disruption, versus ~83% in acute SH fractures.)

    1. Multifocal thickening suggests chronicity

      (Observed in 25% of chronic stress injuries compared to only 3% in acute SH fractures.)

    Results Summary

    • Chronic stress: focal thickening & ZPC discontinuity
    • Acute SH: generalized thickening, hyperintense signal
    • Acute SH: periosteal/perichondrial disruption, edema & effusion
    • Combined markers: 5.7× odds for chronic injury

    Strengths & Weaknesses

    Strengths

    • Robust MRI criteria
    • High inter-observer reliability
    • Clinically relevant findings

    Weaknesses

    • Retrospective design
    • Small sample size
    • Overlapping imaging features
    • Limited to knee & wrist

    Conclusions

    • MRI can differentiate chronic vs. acute physeal injury
    • Focal thickening & ZPC discontinuity favor chronic stress
    • Periosteal/perichondrial injury, edema & effusion indicate SH fracture
    • Combined assessment guides proper treatment
